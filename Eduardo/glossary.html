Training Zones

Zone 1 (Z1) Active Recovery - 0-60% FTP
Very easy spinning, conversational pace. You should be able to talk in complete sentences without effort.
Why it matters: Promotes blood flow and recovery without adding fatigue. Critical for recovery days and warm-up/cool-down periods.
Zone 2 (Z2) Endurance - 60-80% FTP
The foundation of aerobic fitness. Steady, sustainable pace where you can still hold a conversation but with occasional pauses. This is where most training volume should occur.
Why it matters: Builds mitochondrial density, fat oxidation capacity, and aerobic engine. Elite cyclists spend 50-70% of their training time here. It's the foundation that allows you to go hard when it counts.
Zone 3 (Z3) Tempo - 80-90% FTP
Moderately hard, steady effort. Conversation becomes difficult. Also called "sweet spot" (upper Z3, ~88-93% FTP) because it provides substantial stimulus without excessive fatigue.
Why it matters: Improves lactate clearance and muscular endurance. Sweet spot training is particularly effective for time-limited athletes. However, too much Z3 ("grey zone") can limit recovery and prevent hard efforts.
Zone 4 (Z4) Threshold - 90-110% FTP
Sustained hard effort near your lactate threshold. This is your "race pace" for 20-60 minute efforts. Conversation is impossible. Feels uncomfortably hard but sustainable.
Why it matters: Directly improves FTP and time-trial performance. Raises your threshold so you can sustain higher power outputs. Critical for climbing and time trials, but requires 48-72h recovery.
Zone 5 (Z5) VO2 Max - >110% FTP
Very hard intervals typically lasting 2-8 minutes. Maximum sustainable intensity for these durations. Creates significant oxygen debt and burning sensations in legs.
Why it matters: Expands your aerobic ceiling and improves maximum oxygen uptake. Essential for closing gaps, climbing short hills, and responding to attacks. Highly fatiguing - quality over quantity matters.

Power Metrics

FTP Functional Threshold Power
The maximum power you can sustain for approximately 60 minutes in a quasi-steady state. Measured in watts (W). Often expressed relative to body weight (W/kg) for climbing performance.
Why it matters: The single most important cycling metric. It's the anchor for all training zones and the primary measure of aerobic fitness improvement. A higher FTP means you can ride faster and longer.
W/kg Watts per Kilogram
Your power output divided by body weight. Example: 250W FTP √∑ 70kg = 3.57 W/kg.
Why it matters: Predicts climbing performance better than absolute power. On steep climbs, you're fighting gravity, so power-to-weight ratio is everything. 4.0+ W/kg is competitive at local races; 5.0+ is Cat 1/2 level.
NP Normalized Power
A weighted average power that accounts for the physiological cost of power variability. Reflects the "true" physiological stress better than simple average power.
Why it matters: A ride with many surges can have the same average power as a steady ride but feel much harder. NP captures this difference and provides a more accurate measure of training stress.
VI Variability Index
Ratio of Normalized Power to Average Power (NP √∑ AP). A perfectly steady ride has VI = 1.0. Higher values indicate more variable power output.
Why it matters: Measures pacing discipline and efficiency. For endurance rides and time trials, aim for 1.02-1.10. High VI (>1.15) indicates poor pacing or highly variable terrain/efforts, which increases fatigue for the same average power.
Power-Duration Curve PD Curve
Graph showing your best power outputs across different durations (5s, 1min, 5min, 20min, 60min). Reveals your physiological phenotype.
Why it matters: Identifies your strengths and limiters. Are you a sprinter (strong at 5-15s)? Time trialist (strong at 20-60min)? Climber (high W/kg at 5-20min)? Guides training focus and race tactics.

Training Load Metrics

TSS Training Stress Score
Quantifies training stress from a single workout based on intensity and duration. 100 TSS = 1 hour at FTP. A 2-hour endurance ride at 70% FTP = ~100 TSS. A hard 90-minute ride might be 120-150 TSS.
Why it matters: Allows you to quantify and track training load objectively. Helps prevent overtraining (too much TSS too fast) and undertraining (insufficient stimulus). Aim for 300-600 TSS/week depending on fitness level.
CTL Chronic Training Load
Your fitness level, calculated as a 42-day rolling average of daily TSS. Also called "fitness" in TrainingPeaks. Measured in TSS/day.
Why it matters: Tracks your long-term fitness trajectory. Rising CTL means you're building fitness. Dropping CTL means detraining. Competitive cyclists maintain CTL of 80-120 TSS/day. Build CTL gradually (5-8 points per week max).
ATL Acute Training Load
Your fatigue level, calculated as a 7-day rolling average of daily TSS. Also called "fatigue." Measures recent training stress that hasn't been absorbed yet.
Why it matters: High ATL relative to CTL means you're accumulating fatigue faster than building fitness. Used to plan recovery weeks. When ATL is much higher than CTL, performance suffers but adaptations are occurring.
TSB Training Stress Balance
Your freshness/readiness level, calculated as CTL - ATL. Also called "form." Positive TSB = fresh. Negative TSB = fatigued. Zero TSB = balanced.
Why it matters: Predicts race readiness. Optimal TSB for racing: -5 to +5 (slightly fresh). TSB < -20 indicates high overtraining risk. TSB > +10 means you're losing fitness (detraining). Use TSB to time your taper before key events.
ACWR Acute:Chronic Workload Ratio
Ratio of recent training load (7 days) to long-term training load (28 days). Optimal range: 0.8-1.3.
Why it matters: Injury and illness risk increases sharply when ACWR exceeds 1.5 (ramping volume too quickly). Staying in the 0.8-1.3 range minimizes injury risk while allowing progressive overload.

Efficiency Metrics

EF Efficiency Factor
Ratio of Normalized Power to average heart rate (NP √∑ HR). Example: 200W NP at 140 bpm = EF of 1.43.
Why it matters: Rising EF over time indicates improved aerobic efficiency - you're producing more power for the same heart rate (or the same power at lower heart rate). Track EF on steady Z2 rides to monitor base fitness improvements.

Why it matters: Decoupling < 5% on rides ‚â•2 hours indicates strong aerobic base and good endurance. Higher decoupling suggests aerobic system weakness, dehydration, or inadequate fueling. Champions show minimal decoupling even on 4-5 hour rides.
TTE Time to Exhaustion at FTP
How long you can sustain FTP before failure. Well-trained cyclists can hold FTP for 35-50 minutes. Less trained: 20-30 minutes.
Why it matters: TTE is a key limiter for time trials and climbing. You might have decent FTP but poor TTE, meaning you can't utilize your FTP fully in races. Improved through threshold intervals and tempo-over-distance work.
Durability
Your ability to maintain power output late in long rides. Measured by comparing power in final hour vs. first hour of 3+ hour rides.
Why it matters: Races are often decided in the final hour when fatigue is high. Poor durability means your effective FTP drops 20-30% late in rides. Improved through long Z2 rides with "finishers" (hard efforts in final 30-60 min).

Training Concepts

Polarized Training
Intensity distribution where ~80% of training time is easy (Z1-Z2) and ~20% is hard (Z4-Z5+), with minimal time in Z3. Creates a "polarized" distribution with two distinct intensity peaks.
Why it matters: The approach used by most elite endurance athletes. Allows high total volume while maintaining quality in hard sessions. Going easy enough on easy days enables going hard enough on hard days. Reduces injury/burnout risk.
Pyramidal Training
Intensity distribution with most time in Z2, moderate time in Z3/Z4, and small amount in Z5. Forms a pyramid shape when graphed. Example: 70% Z2, 20% Z3/Z4, 10% Z5.
Why it matters: Common among masters/time-limited athletes. More sustainable than threshold-heavy training. Good for building base fitness and maintaining consistency. Less risky than polarized for recovery-limited athletes.
Threshold Training
Heavy focus on Z3-Z4 work (tempo and threshold). Typically 40-50% of training time at moderate-high intensity.
Why it matters: Can rapidly improve FTP in short training blocks (4-8 weeks). However, high injury/burnout risk with prolonged use. Best for short-term FTP gains before events, not year-round. Requires excellent recovery practices.
Periodization
Systematic planning of training into phases (macrocycle, mesocycle, microcycle) with specific goals. Typically: Base ‚Üí Build ‚Üí Peak ‚Üí Recovery ‚Üí Race.
Why it matters: You can't be in peak form year-round. Periodization prevents plateaus, reduces injury risk, and ensures you're fresh for key events. Each phase has specific volume/intensity targets that build upon previous phases.
Deload Week
Planned recovery week with 40-60% reduction in training volume/intensity. Typically every 3-4 weeks. Maintains frequency but reduces duration and intensity.
Why it matters: Adaptations occur during recovery, not during training. Deload weeks allow your body to absorb the previous weeks' training stress, convert it to fitness, and prepare for the next training block. Skipping deloads leads to cumulative fatigue.
Progressive Overload
Gradually increasing training stress over time through higher volume, intensity, or frequency. Typically increase weekly TSS by 5-10% when building fitness.
Why it matters: Your body adapts to current training loads. To improve, you must gradually increase stress. Too fast = injury/overtraining. Too slow = plateau. The 5-10% rule balances adaptation stimulus with recovery capacity.

Performance Metrics

VO2 Max
Maximum rate at which your body can consume oxygen during maximal exercise. Measured in ml/kg/min. Elite cyclists: 70-85 ml/kg/min. Recreational: 40-55 ml/kg/min.
Why it matters: Sets your aerobic ceiling - the upper limit of your endurance capacity. While partly genetic, it's trainable through high-intensity intervals (Z5+). Higher VO2 max enables higher sustainable power outputs.
Lactate Threshold
The exercise intensity where lactate begins accumulating faster than it can be cleared. Closely related to FTP. The primary limiter for sustained efforts.
Why it matters: You can ride just below threshold for hours, but minutes above it. Raising your threshold (via Z3-Z4 training) allows higher sustainable speeds. It's the single most trainable aspect of endurance performance.
Consistency Score
Percentage of planned training sessions completed. Can also measure week-to-week volume variability. High consistency: >85% sessions completed, low weekly TSS variation.
Why it matters: Consistency beats intensity. Missing sessions creates fitness gaps. Erratic volume (big weeks followed by nothing) prevents adaptation. Aim for 4-6 rides/week year-round rather than sporadic blocks of high volume.
Cadence
Pedal revolutions per minute (rpm). Optimal cadence varies by rider but typically 85-95 rpm for endurance, 95-105 rpm for climbing, 75-85 rpm for time trials.
Why it matters: Cadence affects muscular vs. cardiovascular stress. Very low cadence (<70 rpm) increases muscular fatigue and injury risk. Very high cadence (>110 rpm) is cardiovascularly demanding. Find your optimal cadence where power feels sustainable.
